An AI co-founder your business owns.

LeedAB is a local-first AI co-founder for SMBs — a roster of up to 31 specialised agents; each customer gets the subset their business actually needs. The whole stack runs on their own machine and operates the back-office through Telegram.

The pitch "Their data, locally." — three words tight
The Problem

SMBs run on the operational memory of two or three people.

When those people leave, take vacations, or get overwhelmed, the business stalls. AI agents can absorb that operational memory — but only if it lives in a place the customer owns and can audit. Cloud-AI products lose that trust. Local-AI products do not.

1

Vendor lock-in

Cloud AI products hold customer data in proprietary stores. When the vendor pivots, prices, or breaks — customer is stuck or starts over. SMBs have heard this story too many times in 2026.

2

Privacy & compliance

Logistics, professional services, regulated trades — every SMB has data that contractually cannot leave their network. Cloud AI doesn't pass procurement at most of them.

3

State fragmentation

Customer runs Xero + ServiceM8 + Gmail + Notion. Each tool keeps its own state; the operator stitches them together by hand. The "AI" in each is shallow because it can only see its own slice.

What we make

Up to thirty-one agents — pick the subset your business actually needs.

Customer's team talks to one bot on Telegram. A COO orchestrator routes to the licensed sub-agents — typically 5 to 15 of the canonical roster, selected per-customer via the license-key manifest. A solo legal practice gets a different subset than a 30-person logistics company. Cross-cutting questions have a home; cost, latency, and isolation are enforced centrally. Memory lives in a per-business Obsidian vault on the customer's hardware — they own it, can audit it, can leave with it.

Three claims, ranked by defensibility
1

Local-first by design — "their data, locally"

LeedAB OS installs on the customer's own machine. Customer data never leaves except as Anthropic API request/response — and even then only prompts go out, not raw business data. Most AI-agent products invert this. We don't.

2

Per-business Obsidian brain as the nervous system

Every customer gets a markdown vault on their hardware that all their agents read and write. State doesn't fragment across SaaS tools. The customer can fork it, audit it, leave with it. Markdown is durable. The customer-owned brain is the actual moat.

3

Single-bot routing via a COO orchestrator

One conversational surface per customer. The COO delegates to sub-agents and synthesises one reply. Five reliability guarantees enforced centrally: every inbound gets a reply, no hallucinated answers, no cross-customer context leaks, cost cap per orchestration, audit log of every delegation.

The Moat

If a competitor copies our agents next week, what do we still have?

The agents are copyable in days. The orchestrator is copyable in weeks. The channels are copyable in days. The customer's installed brain is copyable never — their data is theirs. The trust built by letting them leave easily IS the lock-in. Counter-intuitive, but real.

Why now

Three converging waves in 2026.

i.

Tool-use is finally good enough

Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.7 can drive a real workflow without constant supervision. Two years ago the loops broke; now they don't. The capability landed in 2026.

ii.

Consumer hardware runs production AI

Modern Mac Mini M-series and UGREEN AI NAS match cloud performance for the agent loop at consumer prices. Five years ago this required a server rack. Now it sits on a desk.

iii.

SMBs are skeptical of cloud AI

Privacy reporting, lock-in horror stories, and AI-vendor pivots have made local-first land in 2026 in a way it didn't in 2022. Buyers ask "but does it leak my data?" before "but does it work?".
